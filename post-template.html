The Meeting That Should've Been an Email (And Why We Still Schedule It)

We've all said it. We've all lived it. So why does it keep happening?

Back to all posts

Let me paint you a picture. You're in the middle of something — actually focused, genuinely in flow — when a calendar notification slides in. A thirty-minute sync. No agenda. Just a title that says "Quick Catch-Up."

You've been here before. You know this meeting. Twelve minutes of small talk, six minutes of status updates that could've been a bullet point, and twelve more minutes of someone screen-sharing a spreadsheet nobody asked to see. Then it ends, and everyone agrees to "circle back."

"The meeting that should've been an email isn't just a productivity meme. It's a symptom of something deeper about how we signal work, presence, and belonging."

What's interesting is that virtual workers — people who fought hard for the flexibility of remote life — seem just as prone to this as anyone in an office. Sometimes more so. And I've spent a while trying to figure out why.

The Real Reason We Schedule Unnecessary Meetings

It's not laziness, and it's not ignorance. Most people know, somewhere in their gut, when a meeting isn't necessary. The real drivers are a bit more uncomfortable to name.

1. Visibility anxiety

When you're remote, you're invisible by default. A meeting is one of the few ways to prove you exist, that you're engaged, that you're contributing. Scheduling one — or agreeing to one — can feel like a safe way to demonstrate presence without actually having to articulate your value in writing.

2. The comfort of synchronous thinking

Some people genuinely process better in conversation than in text. For them, a meeting isn't inefficient — it's how they think. The problem is that these individuals often don't realise their preference comes at a cost to the six other people in the room who would've preferred an email.

3. Decision avoidance

This one is underrated. When nobody wants to be the person who made the call, a meeting becomes a mechanism for collective ambiguity. No one decides in the meeting, but at least no one can be blamed for deciding alone.

The Asymmetry Nobody Talks About

Here's the thing about a meeting versus an email: an email is asynchronous. The burden of interruption is low. The reader decides when to engage. A meeting, on the other hand, commandeers everyone's time simultaneously — and the value extraction is wildly unequal.

The person who called the meeting usually gets the most out of it. Everyone else is supporting cast.

A few frameworks I've found actually useful:

  • Default to async. If it can be a message, it should be a message. If it can be a shared doc with comments, it should be that.
  • Require an agenda. No agenda, no meeting. This sounds harsh but it filters out a surprising number of sessions that dissolve on their own when someone has to articulate the point.
  • Set a decision rule. Every meeting should have a clear answer to: what gets decided here, and by whom?
  • Make it easy to say no. Build a culture where declining a meeting isn't rude — it's efficient.

My Honest Take

I don't think unnecessary meetings are a moral failure. They're a social one. We schedule them because the alternatives — writing clearly, making decisions, being direct — are harder. A meeting is the path of least resistance when the real work is communication.

The virtual workers who've figured this out aren't necessarily more disciplined. They've just been burned enough times to value their own time loudly enough that others respect it.

Start there. Be inconveniently protective of your focused hours. The calendar invites will get better.
