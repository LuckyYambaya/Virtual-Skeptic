Time for Companies Abroad to Cut the Middlemen

On direct hiring Filipino talents and what it actually costs to hire from an agency.

Agencies hiring Filipino workers on your behalf are charging you double — and pocketing the difference every single month.

The spread between what you pay and what the worker earns? Anywhere from 50% to 100%. That's not a service fee. That's a recurring tax on someone else's labor.

That's not a service fee. That's a recurring tax on someone else's labor.

The business model is simple: a company abroad pays a premium rate, the agency pays the Filipino worker a fraction of that, and they earn off the arbitrage. If companies were willing to spend that money anyway, it could be genuinely life-changing for workers. Many could be lifted out of poverty within a year. Instead, that money disappears into agency margins.

To be fair…

Agencies are doing a lot. Headhunting, recruitment, onboarding, payroll, compliance, HR support, replacement guarantees — that's real work.

And for companies with no international hiring experience, it can feel like a safety net. There are also real risks on the worker's side: direct clients can ghost mid-project, payments get messy without a third party, and not every foreign employer knows how to manage remote workers professionally. Some agencies genuinely advocate for their people, offer benefits, and ensure fair rates.

But a lot of them are simply in the way.

Because the internet has already made their core function unnecessary. LinkedIn lets you post jobs visible to applicants in the Philippines. Indeed has been surfacing Philippine-based talent to global listings for years. Hubstaff Talent is a free directory built specifically for remote hiring. The infrastructure is there — you just haven't used it yet.

More companies from the US, Canada, the UK, and Australia are realizing this. They're posting directly, messaging directly, onboarding directly. And Filipino workers are finally walking away with rates that actually reflect the value of their work — not whatever's left after the agency takes its cut.

How to Actually Do It

  • 1

    Post the job

    Put your listing on LinkedIn (filter by Philippines location and role) or Jobstreet.com.ph for more traditional roles. If you want to browse without posting, Hubstaff Talent is free and lets you search profiles directly. For senior or technical roles, go straight to LinkedIn and headhunt — message candidates directly.

  • 2

    Screen and interview

    Use Calendly to let shortlisted candidates book their own interview slots. Run the interview on Zoom or Google Meet. For async screening (especially useful across time zones), ask them to record a 3-minute walkthrough of how they'd approach a real task or any question you might have.

  • 3

    Lock in the contract

    Use ChatGPT or Claude to draft a straightforward contractor agreement. Use HelloSign or DocuSign for signing. Keep it simple: scope, rate, hours, confidentiality, termination terms.

  • 4

    Set up payroll

    Wise (formerly TransferWise) is the simplest option — low fees, fast transfers, and your worker receives PHP directly into their local bank account. PayPal and Payoneer work too, though fees are higher. You can also send directly to a USD bank account.

  • 5

    Onboard with clarity

    Set up a shared Notion or Google Doc with your tools, workflows, communication norms, and what the first 30 days look like. Use Loom to record short walkthrough videos of your systems. Add them to your Slack workspace and introduce them to the team properly. Filipino workers are fast learners, but they need a clear map upfront.

  • 6

    Manage day-to-day

    Slack for communication. Asana, Trello, or ClickUp for task management — pick whichever your team already uses. Output matters more than hours logged. Set a standing weekly 30-minute check-in on the calendar and protect it. That one touchpoint prevents 90% of remote work problems before they start.

  • 7

    Retain them

    Pay on time, every time — this is non-negotiable. Give a performance review at the 6-month mark with a raise baked in if they've delivered. Beyond money: give feedback, recognize good work publicly in Slack, and include them in team decisions where relevant.
