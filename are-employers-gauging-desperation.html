Are Employers Gauging Your Desperation?

On lengthy hiring processes, unpaid tasks, and the art of lowballing Filipino freelancers.

I just spent three days completing a "pre-screening task" for a company I never heard back from. THREE DAYS!

Here's what happened:

My full-time job ends in 7 days. So I'm applying for remote positions, and I just found one. A marketing coordinator / copywriter job advertised by a foreign agency looking for Filipino talent.

The job post said the pay was "competitive." I should've known then.

The first email asked me to fill out a lengthy application form, beyond what my resume already covered. Fine. The second asked for a writing sample on a specific topic they outlined. A little unusual, but doable. The third asked me to complete a timed assessment. By the fourth email asking for a full content strategy draft, I started to wonder.

At what point does the hiring process end and the actual job begin?

Frustrated, I still finished the task and submitted it. Then came one final form — a "culture fit" questionnaire. Buried at the bottom, almost like an afterthought, was a field asking if I was amenable to a starting salary that would make a fresh grad wince.

I closed the tab. Took a breath, and reconsidered my life choices.

After going through the 5 stages of grieving, I received another one of these tasks. And it dawned on me. They're using this as a filter. The more tasks you complete without pushing back, the more you reveal about how badly you need the job.

And once they know that, the offer they send you won't be based on what the job is worth. It'll be based on what they think you'll accept.

Sunk Cost.

The more hoops a candidate jumps through, the harder it becomes to walk away. By the time an offer finally lands (usually a lowball offer) you have already invested so much time and energy that saying no feels like a loss.

They're basically betting that your sunk costs will do the convincing for them.

To be fair…

Not every multi-stage hiring process is predatory.

Remote employers carry real risks. They can't meet candidates in person, and plenty have been burned by people who looked great on paper and ghosted them after onboarding. So a structured evaluation makes sense. Some tasks can effectively reveal whether someone can do the work.

And yes, Filipino freelancers are in high demand precisely because they are talented, communicative, and hardworking. Agencies know this.

They also know that most of them are desperate.

I just want to work. Do the things I'm good at, get paid fairly for it, and not spend an entire week auditioning for something that was never real.

This isn't too much to ask, especially from companies that claim to have integrity.

And for the agencies reading this:

I know that the uncertainty is real, but there are better ways to deal with it.

  • 1

    Live verbal + written test.

    On a 30-minute call, present real scenarios and ask how they would approach them. Follow it up with a live writing task. You get to see how they think and communicate without dragging the process out.

  • 2

    Paid trial projects.

    Give a small task and pay them for it. You get to see their actual work. They get to see how you operate. Everyone walks away with something.

  • 3

    Short-term contracts.

    Hire them for two to four weeks. See how they communicate, how they handle feedback, whether they deliver. Then decide. It costs a little more upfront and saves significantly more in the long run.

  • 4

    Hire fast, fire faster.

    Lower the barrier to entry, shorten the probation period, and make the exit process clean. Speed replaces the illusion of certainty that long hiring processes provide. Besides, no amount of pre-screening will tell you what two weeks of actual work will.

  • 5

    Be transparent about pay.

    A candidate who knows the rate upfront and still applies is a genuinely interested one. Hiding the number behind "competitive" doesn't protect your budget. It just filters out people with healthy self-worth.

Fix the process, and you might be surprised who shows up and sticks around.
